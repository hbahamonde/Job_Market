\section*{1. Profiling Document (Summary of Teaching Competence and Fit)}

I am a political scientist (PhD, U.S.) and Senior Researcher at the INVEST Research Flagship Centre. My teaching profile is defined by two strengths: (i) rigorous training in quantitative and experimental methods for social science, and (ii) substantive integration of political economy, inequality, and institutions with research design. I teach at undergraduate, MA, and PhD levels, and I am prepared to contribute to OsloMet's quantitative social science curriculum through methods instruction, supervision, and course development.

\paragraph{Core teaching competence.}
I have taught quantitative methods (OLS and maximum likelihood), causal inference, and experimental social science in multiple institutional contexts (Finland, Chile, and the United States). In methods courses, I emphasize research design, identification, and reproducible workflows. Students learn to translate theory into testable implications, choose estimators consistent with the data-generating process, and communicate uncertainty and limitations clearly. In substantive courses (comparative politics, political economy, democracy, and inequality), I integrate empirical illustrations so that conceptual claims are consistently linked to evidence.

\paragraph{Teaching approach and pedagogy.}
My teaching philosophy is grounded in building durable analytical judgment. I combine structured explanation with learn-by-doing pedagogy: frequent short exercises, guided replication tasks, and iterative writing and analysis assignments. Assessment is designed to support learning rather than merely rank performance: I use clear rubrics, transparent grading criteria, and multiple opportunities for feedback. I also employ predictable discussion formats and multiple channels of participation to support inclusive classrooms.

\paragraph{Supervision and mentoring.}
I view supervision as a core teaching responsibility, particularly in quantitative social science. I support students in question formulation, measurement, research design, and analysis planning, and I encourage preregistration and open materials when appropriate. Currently, I mentor one master's student and supervise two PhD-level students as main supervisor. I am prepared to supervise MA theses and PhD dissertations that rely on experiments, panel data, and observational causal inference.

\paragraph{Courses I can contribute to and develop.}
I am prepared to teach courses in applied quantitative methods (OLS, MLE, causal inference), experimental methods, and research design at MA and PhD levels. I can also contribute substantively to courses on comparative political economy, inequality, democratic resilience, and institutions. In addition, I am prepared to develop a graduate-level course on reproducible quantitative workflows and causal identification (preregistration, simulation-based intuition, and transparent reporting), tailored to OsloMet's programs.

\section*{2. Teaching Statement}

\paragraph{Teaching philosophy.}
My teaching philosophy is grounded in helping students develop durable analytical judgment. Political science exposes students to competing theories, methodological pluralism, and normatively charged debates. My role as an instructor is therefore to serve as a structured guide: clarifying core questions, demonstrating how credible evidence is produced and evaluated, and training students to move from description to causal inference and, when feasible, to mechanism-based explanations.

\paragraph{Teaching practice.}
Across undergraduate, MA, and PhD classrooms, I emphasize learn-by-doing pedagogy. Students practice the tasks political scientists actually perform: formulating research questions, deriving testable implications from theory, designing empirical strategies, and communicating results clearly. I rely on frequent low-stakes assessments, structured discussion formats, and transparent grading criteria to ensure that all students can engage meaningfully with the material.

\paragraph{Substantive and methodological integration.}
I deliberately integrate substantive content (comparative politics, political economy, democracy, inequality, and development) with research design and quantitative reasoning. In substantive courses, students work with empirical illustrations to understand how arguments translate into evidence. In methods courses, technical tools are always anchored in substantive problems, so that estimation is never treated as an abstract exercise.

\paragraph{Mentoring and academic community.}
Teaching extends beyond the classroom. I view mentoring and advising as integral to pedagogy, particularly at the graduate level. I actively support students in developing research ideas, improving analytical clarity, and navigating academic career decisions. I also value collegial teaching environments and actively contribute to coherent curricula across degree levels. Currently, I mentor one master's degree student, and two PhD-level students as their main supervisor.

\paragraph{Teaching interests.}
I am prepared to teach courses in comparative politics, political economy of development, democracy and democratic backsliding, inequality, state formation, Latin American politics, and applied quantitative and experimental methods, at both undergraduate and graduate levels.
