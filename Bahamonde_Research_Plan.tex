\section*{1. Research Agenda and Core Contribution}

My research investigates how democratic accountability erodes not only through elite manipulation but also through mass preferences that tolerate, enable, or demand non-electoral forms of governance. Substantively, I focus on democratic backsliding, technocratic delegation, inequality, and clientelism. Conceptually, my work centers on \textbf{losers’ consent}: the conditions under which electoral losers accept outcomes, institutions, and authority arrangements that weaken electoral control.

Across this agenda, I develop a unified theoretical perspective in which citizens evaluate democratic arrangements as \emph{trade-offs} between ideological representation, material outcomes, and perceived performance advantages. This approach integrates political economy, political psychology, and formal theory, and is implemented using survey experiments, laboratory experiments, and observational data.

Over the next four years, my research will consolidate this agenda into a coherent program that explains when voters accept technocracy, tolerate democratic transgressions, or strategically engage in non-programmatic exchanges.

\section*{2. Ongoing Research Streams}

\subsection*{A. Losers’ Consent, Technocracy, and Democratic Backsliding}

Building on my recent work on technocratic delegation and democratic norms, this stream examines how ideological distance from governing coalitions shapes support for expert rule, independent institutions, and non-majoritarian governance. The central theoretical claim is that delegation is conditional: voters accept expert authority when perceived performance gains outweigh ideological losses, and reject it when experts are seen as aligned with incumbents.

Planned extensions include:
\begin{itemize}
  \item Comparative survey experiments across Nordic and European democracies.
  \item Experimental manipulations of expert neutrality, institutional alignment, and crisis framing.
  \item Formal models linking ideological distance, perceived competence, and delegation preferences.
\end{itemize}

This stream speaks directly to debates on democratic backsliding, technocracy, and institutional trust, and targets journals such as \emph{AJPS}, \emph{APSR}, and \emph{Comparative Political Studies}.

\subsection*{B. Inequality, Risk, and Anti-Systemic Politics}

A second stream studies how economic inequality reshapes political behavior through loss aversion and risk sensitivity. Drawing on prospect theory, I analyze how voters exposed to economic losses become more willing to support populist, anti-systemic, or authoritarian alternatives, even when these undermine democratic accountability.

Future work will:
\begin{itemize}
  \item Combine longitudinal survey experiments with macro-economic shocks.
  \item Integrate affective polarization and identity substitution mechanisms.
  \item Extend analyses beyond voters to include political candidates and elites.
\end{itemize}

This research contributes to political economy accounts of populism and democratic erosion and builds bridges between inequality research and political psychology.

\subsection*{C. Clientelism, Vote Buying, and Vote Selling}

My third research stream reorients the study of clientelism by modeling voters as strategic actors rather than passive recipients. Using formal theory and laboratory experiments, I analyze how institutional rules determining who initiates exchanges (parties vs voters) reshape targeting strategies and surplus distribution.

Over the next four years, I will:
\begin{itemize}
  \item Extend laboratory designs to comparative and online settings.
  \item Study how electoral risk and uncertainty condition strategic vote selling.
  \item Link clientelism to broader questions of democratic accountability and inequality.
\end{itemize}

This line of work advances experimental political economy and contributes to debates on non-programmatic representation.

% =================================================
\section*{3. Methods and Research Design}

Methodologically, my research combines:
\begin{itemize}
  \item Formal theoretical modeling to clarify mechanisms.
  \item Survey and laboratory experiments to identify causal effects.
  \item Observational and register data to assess external validity.
\end{itemize}

A key priority is addressing social desirability bias and endogeneity through experimental designs and longitudinal data. I also emphasize replication, open science practices, and pre-registration.

\section*{4. Four-Year Plan and Outputs (2026--2029)}

\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{Year 1--2:} Completion and publication of core papers on technocratic delegation and democratic backsliding; development of comparative extensions.
  \item \textbf{Year 2--3:} New experimental projects on inequality, risk, and anti-systemic voting; initiation of comparative data collection.
  \item \textbf{Year 3--4:} Integration of research streams into a unified theoretical framework; preparation of a monograph or synthetic articles.
\end{itemize}

Expected outputs include 6--8 peer-reviewed journal articles, collaborative grant applications, and policy-facing publications.
